Part (c) - Accounting Rate of Return (6 marks)

Project SolarMax:

Annual Depreciation = (3,600,000 - 400,000) / 5 = £640,000
Total Cash Flows = 650,000 + 1,100,000 + 1,250,000 + 850,000 + 750,000 = £4,600,000

Average Annual Profit = 1,400,000 / 5 = £280,000
Average Investment = (3,600,000 + 400,000) / 2 = £2,000,000

Project WindPower:

Part (d) - Conflicting Recommendations (8 marks)

Why methods may conflict:

  1. Time value of money: Payback and ARR ignore the time value of money, while NPV incorporates discounting. This can lead to different rankings.
  2. Cash flows vs accounting profits: NPV and Payback use cash flows; ARR uses accounting profits (after depreciation). Different bases produce different results.
  3. Project scale: Smaller projects may have faster payback but lower absolute returns. NPV favours projects that add more value regardless of speed.
  4. Cash flow timing: Payback favours early cash flows; NPV considers the entire project life. A project with slower early returns but higher later returns may rank differently.

Resolution: NPV should be the primary criterion as it directly measures shareholder wealth creation. Payback can be used as a secondary liquidity/risk measure. ARR should be used cautiously due to its reliance on accounting figures rather than cash flows.

SECTION B - ANSWER ANY TWO QUESTIONS (15 Marks Each)

Question 2 - Time Value of Money & Bonds

Atlantic Industries Plc is considering raising capital through a bond issue. The company plans to issue 50,000 bonds, each with a face value of £1,000, a coupon rate of 6% paid annually, and a maturity of 8 years. The current market yield for similar bonds is 7.5%.

Required:

  1. Calculate the current market price of one bond. (5 marks)
  2. Calculate the total amount of capital the company will raise from the bond issue. (2 marks)
  3. Explain the relationship between coupon rate, yield to maturity, and bond price. Include in your answer why this bond is trading at a premium or discount. (4 marks)
  4. An investor is considering a zero-coupon bond with a face value of £1,000, maturing in 10 years, currently priced at £558. Calculate the yield to maturity of this bond. (4 marks)

Total: 15 Marks

Model Answer - Question 2

Part (a) - Bond Price (5 marks)

Annual Coupon = £1,000 × 6% = £60

Bond Price = PV of coupons + PV of face value
= 60 × PVIFA(7.5%, 8) + 1,000 × PVIF(7.5%, 8)

PVIFA(7.5%, 8) = [1 - (1.075)^-8] / 0.075 = 5.8573
PVIF(7.5%, 8) = 1 / (1.075)^8 = 0.5607

Bond Price = 60 × 5.8573 + 1,000 × 0.5607
= 351.44 + 560.70
= £912.14

Part (b) - Total Capital Raised (2 marks)

Total Capital = 50,000 bonds × £912.14
= £45,607,000

Part (c) - Relationship Explanation (4 marks)

The relationship between coupon rate, YTM, and bond price:

  • When coupon rate > YTM: Bond trades at a premium (above face value)
  • When coupon rate < YTM: Bond trades at a discount (below face value)
  • When coupon rate = YTM: Bond trades at par (face value)

In this case, the coupon rate (6%) is less than the YTM (7.5%), so the bond trades at a discount (£912.14 < £1,000). Investors require higher returns than the coupon provides, so they pay less than face value to achieve the market yield.

Part (d) - Zero-Coupon Bond YTM (4 marks)

For zero-coupon bonds: Price = Face Value / (1 + YTM)^n

558 = 1,000 / (1 + YTM)^10
(1 + YTM)^10 = 1,000 / 558 = 1.7921
1 + YTM = (1.7921)^(1/10) = 1.0600
YTM = 6.00%

Question 3 - Risk, Return & CAPM

You are a portfolio manager at Meridian Capital. A client is considering investing in two stocks: TechGrowth Inc. and StableDiv Corp. Historical data shows:

Stock Expected Return Standard Deviation Beta
TechGrowth Inc. 18% 35% 1.6
StableDiv Corp. 10% 15% 0.7

The current risk-free rate is 4% and the expected market return is 11%.

Required:

  1. Using the Capital Asset Pricing Model (CAPM), calculate the required return for each stock. (4 marks)
  2. Based on your calculations in (a), determine whether each stock is overvalued, undervalued, or fairly valued. Explain your reasoning. (4 marks)
  3. Explain the difference between systematic and unsystematic risk. Why is only systematic risk rewarded in the market? (4 marks)
  4. Discuss one limitation of using beta as a measure of risk. (3 marks)

Total: 15 Marks

Model Answer - Question 3

Part (a) - CAPM Required Returns (4 marks)

CAPM: r = Rf + β(Rm - Rf)

TechGrowth Inc.:
r = 4% + 1.6 × (11% - 4%)
r = 4% + 1.6 × 7%
r = 4% + 11.2%
Required Return = 15.2%

StableDiv Corp.:
r = 4% + 0.7 × (11% - 4%)
r = 4% + 0.7 × 7%
r = 4% + 4.9%
Required Return = 8.9%

Part (b) - Valuation Analysis (4 marks)

TechGrowth Inc.:

  • Expected Return: 18%
  • Required Return (CAPM): 15.2%
  • Since Expected > Required, the stock is UNDERVALUED (good buy)

StableDiv Corp.:

  • Expected Return: 10%
  • Required Return (CAPM): 8.9%
  • Since Expected > Required, the stock is UNDERVALUED (good buy)

Both stocks offer returns above what CAPM suggests is fair compensation for their risk levels, making them attractive investments.

Part (c) - Systematic vs Unsystematic Risk (4 marks)

Systematic Risk: Market-wide risk that affects all securities (e.g., interest rate changes, inflation, recessions, geopolitical events). Cannot be eliminated through diversification. Measured by beta.

Unsystematic Risk: Company-specific or industry-specific risk (e.g., management changes, product failures, labor strikes). Can be reduced or eliminated through diversification.

Why only systematic risk is rewarded: In efficient markets, investors are assumed to hold diversified portfolios that eliminate unsystematic risk. Since unsystematic risk can be "diversified away" at no cost, the market does not compensate investors for bearing it. Only systematic risk, which cannot be avoided, commands a risk premium.

Part (d) - Limitation of Beta (3 marks)

Historical nature: Beta is calculated using historical data, which assumes past relationships between the stock and market will continue. However, companies change over time (new products, acquisitions, industry shifts), so historical beta may not accurately predict future risk.

Alternative answers accepted: Beta assumes linear relationship; ignores other risk factors; sensitive to measurement period; assumes market portfolio is observable.

Question 4 - Capital Structure & Dividend Policy

Phoenix Manufacturing Ltd is reviewing its capital structure and dividend policy. The company currently has:

  • 2 million ordinary shares trading at £12 per share
  • The most recent dividend was £0.80 per share, with expected constant growth of 5% per annum
  • £10 million in corporate bonds with a coupon rate of 8% and current yield of 6%
  • Corporate tax rate: 25%

Required:

  1. Calculate the company's Weighted Average Cost of Capital (WACC). (6 marks)
  2. Explain the Modigliani and Miller dividend irrelevance theory and state TWO assumptions that must hold for this theory to apply. (5 marks)
  3. Discuss TWO reasons why, in practice, dividend policy may matter to shareholders. (4 marks)

Total: 15 Marks

Model Answer - Question 4

Part (a) - WACC Calculation (6 marks)

Step 1: Market Values
Market Value of Equity (E) = 2,000,000 × £12 = £24,000,000
Market Value of Debt (D) = £10,000,000
Total Value (V) = £24,000,000 + £10,000,000 = £34,000,000

Step 2: Weights
Weight of Equity (E/V) = 24,000,000 / 34,000,000 = 0.7059 (70.59%)
Weight of Debt (D/V) = 10,000,000 / 34,000,000 = 0.2941 (29.41%)

Step 3: Cost of Equity (Dividend Growth Model)
D1 = D0 × (1 + g) = £0.80 × 1.05 = £0.84
Ke = (D1 / P0) + g = (0.84 / 12) + 0.05 = 0.07 + 0.05 = 12%

Step 4: After-tax Cost of Debt
Kd(1-T) = 6% × (1 - 0.25) = 4.5%

Step 5: WACC
WACC = (E/V × Ke) + (D/V × Kd(1-T))
WACC = (0.7059 × 12%) + (0.2941 × 4.5%)
WACC = 8.47% + 1.32%
WACC = 9.79%

Part (b) - M&M Dividend Irrelevance (5 marks)

Theory: Modigliani and Miller (1961) argue that in perfect capital markets, dividend policy is irrelevant to firm value. The value of a company depends solely on its investment decisions and earning power, not on how it distributes profits. Shareholders can create "homemade dividends" by selling shares if they want income, or reinvest dividends if they prefer capital gains.

Key Assumptions (any 2):

  • No taxes (or equal taxation of dividends and capital gains)
  • No transaction costs
  • No flotation costs for new share issues
  • Perfect capital markets with symmetric information
  • Rational investors
  • No agency costs

Part (c) - Why Dividend Policy Matters (4 marks)

Reason 1 - Signalling Effect: Dividends convey information about management's confidence in future earnings. A dividend increase signals strong future prospects, while a cut may indicate financial difficulties. Since managers have better information than investors (information asymmetry), dividend changes affect share prices.

Reason 2 - Tax Clientele Effect: Different investors have different tax preferences. Some investors (e.g., pension funds, tax-exempt institutions) prefer dividends, while others prefer capital gains due to lower tax rates or deferral. Companies attract investors whose preferences match their dividend policy.

Alternative answers accepted: Bird-in-hand theory (certainty of dividends vs uncertain capital gains); agency costs (dividends reduce free cash flow that managers might waste); liquidity preferences.
