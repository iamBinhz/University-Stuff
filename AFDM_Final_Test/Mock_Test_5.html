Part (d) - Limitations of IRR (4 marks)

Limitation 1 - Reinvestment Rate Assumption:

IRR assumes that all intermediate cash flows are reinvested at the IRR itself. This is often unrealistic, especially for projects with very high IRRs. NPV more realistically assumes reinvestment at the cost of capital.

Limitation 2 - Multiple IRRs:

Projects with non-conventional cash flows (multiple sign changes) can have multiple IRRs or no real IRR, making interpretation difficult or impossible. This occurs when cash flows switch between positive and negative multiple times during the project life.

Other valid limitations: Does not consider project scale; may conflict with NPV for mutually exclusive projects; assumes flat yield curve.

Part (e) - NPV vs IRR Ranking Conflicts (4 marks)

NPV and IRR may give different rankings for mutually exclusive projects due to:

1. Scale differences: A smaller project may have a higher IRR but a lower NPV than a larger project. IRR measures percentage return while NPV measures absolute value creation.

2. Timing of cash flows: Projects with early cash flows are favoured by IRR (due to the reinvestment assumption), while NPV evaluates all cash flows at the cost of capital. This can cause different rankings when comparing projects with different cash flow patterns.

Resolution: When conflicts occur, NPV should be preferred as it directly measures shareholder wealth creation.

Question 2 - Bonds and Debt Financing

Oceanic Industries Plc has two bonds currently trading in the market:

Bond A: 8% annual coupon bond, face value £1,000, 5 years to maturity, currently priced at £1,042.

Bond B: 6% semi-annual coupon bond, face value £1,000, 8 years to maturity, currently priced at £925.

Required:

  1. For Bond A, calculate the current yield and explain whether the bond is trading at a premium or discount and why. (3 marks)
  2. For Bond B, calculate the bond price if the yield to maturity is 7% per annum (compounded semi-annually). (5 marks)
  3. Explain the relationship between bond prices and interest rates. What happens to bond prices when market interest rates rise? (3 marks)
  4. Discuss TWO factors that affect the credit risk of a corporate bond. (4 marks)

Total: 15 Marks

Model Answer - Question 2

Part (a) - Current Yield and Premium/Discount (3 marks)

Current Yield:
Current Yield = Annual Coupon / Current Price
= (£1,000 × 8%) / £1,042
= £80 / £1,042
= 7.68%

Premium or Discount:

Bond A is trading at a PREMIUM (£1,042 > £1,000 face value).

Reason: The coupon rate (8%) is higher than the current market yield (7.68%). Investors are willing to pay more than face value because the bond offers higher coupon payments than currently available market rates. The premium compensates for the higher-than-market interest payments.

Part (b) - Semi-Annual Bond Price (5 marks)

Adjustments for Semi-Annual:
Annual coupon rate = 6%, so semi-annual coupon = 6% / 2 = 3%
Semi-annual coupon payment = £1,000 × 3% = £30
Annual YTM = 7%, so semi-annual YTM = 7% / 2 = 3.5%
Number of periods = 8 years × 2 = 16 periods

Bond Price Calculation:
P = C × PVIFA(r,n) + FV × PVIF(r,n)

PVIFA(3.5%, 16) = [1 - (1.035)^-16] / 0.035
= [1 - 0.5767] / 0.035 = 0.4233 / 0.035 = 12.094

PVIF(3.5%, 16) = 1 / (1.035)^16 = 0.5767

P = 30 × 12.094 + 1,000 × 0.5767
P = 362.82 + 576.70
P = £939.52

Part (c) - Bond Prices and Interest Rates (3 marks)

Bond prices and interest rates have an inverse relationship:

  • When market interest rates RISE, bond prices FALL
  • When market interest rates FALL, bond prices RISE

Explanation: When market rates rise, newly issued bonds offer higher coupon payments. Existing bonds with lower fixed coupons become less attractive, so their prices must fall to offer competitive yields. Conversely, when rates fall, existing bonds with higher fixed coupons become more valuable.

Part (d) - Factors Affecting Credit Risk (4 marks)

Factor 1 - Company's Financial Health:

The issuing company's profitability, cash flow stability, and debt levels affect its ability to make interest and principal payments. Key metrics include interest coverage ratio, debt-to-equity ratio, and operating cash flows. Companies with weak financials have higher default risk and thus higher credit risk.

Factor 2 - Industry and Economic Conditions:

Companies in cyclical industries (e.g., construction, automotive) face higher credit risk during economic downturns. Recession increases default probability across many sectors. The overall economic environment directly impacts a company's revenue and ability to service debt.

Other valid factors: Company size/diversification; quality of management; existing debt covenants; collateral/security; credit rating history.

Part (b) - Three Forms of Market Efficiency (6 marks)

1. Weak-Form Efficiency:

Share prices reflect all past price and trading volume information. Historical data is already incorporated into current prices. Implication: Technical analysis (studying charts and patterns) cannot consistently generate abnormal returns because past price movements do not predict future movements.

2. Semi-Strong Form Efficiency:

Share prices reflect all publicly available information, including financial statements, news announcements, economic data, and analyst reports. Prices adjust rapidly when new public information is released. Implication: Fundamental analysis cannot consistently beat the market because all public information is already reflected in prices.

3. Strong-Form Efficiency:

Share prices reflect ALL information, both public and private (insider information). Even corporate insiders cannot consistently earn abnormal returns. Implication: No investor can beat the market, even with access to privileged information. This form is generally considered too extreme; insider trading laws exist because insiders can exploit private information.

Part (c) - Implications for Active Investors (5 marks)

Implications for Technical Analysis:

If markets are at least weak-form efficient, technical analysis is futile. Chart patterns, moving averages, and trading signals based on historical prices cannot predict future returns because all past information is already reflected in current prices. Technical traders would, on average, earn only normal returns minus transaction costs.

Implications for Fundamental Analysis:

If markets are semi-strong form efficient, fundamental analysis (analyzing financial statements, valuation ratios, competitive position) cannot consistently generate abnormal returns. By the time an analyst identifies an "undervalued" stock, the information is likely already priced in. The analyst's research costs would reduce returns below market average.

Overall Implication:

If EMH holds, investors should consider passive investment strategies (index funds) rather than paying high fees for active management. However, markets may not be perfectly efficient - behavioral biases, information processing costs, and market frictions may create opportunities for skilled investors, though these are difficult to exploit consistently after costs.

Question 4 - Dividend Policy and Share Valuation

Crystal Technologies Plc is a mature technology company considering changes to its dividend policy. The company currently has:

  • Net income of £50 million
  • 20 million shares outstanding
  • Current share price of £15
  • Current dividend payout ratio of 40%

The board is debating whether to increase the payout ratio to 60% or use the extra funds for a share buyback program.

Required:

  1. Calculate the current dividend per share (DPS) and the dividend yield. (3 marks)
  2. If the company increases the payout ratio to 60%, calculate the new DPS. If retained earnings are expected to grow at 8% and the required return is 12%, calculate the share value using the dividend growth model. State any assumptions. (5 marks)
  3. As an alternative to dividends, explain how a share buyback returns value to shareholders. What are TWO advantages of buybacks over dividends? (4 marks)
  4. Explain the signalling effect of dividend changes and why managers are often reluctant to cut dividends. (3 marks)

Total: 15 Marks

Model Answer - Question 4

Part (a) - Current DPS and Dividend Yield (3 marks)

Earnings Per Share (EPS):
EPS = Net Income / Shares Outstanding
= £50,000,000 / 20,000,000
= £2.50 per share

Current Dividend Per Share:
DPS = EPS × Payout Ratio
= £2.50 × 40%
= £1.00 per share

Dividend Yield:
Dividend Yield = DPS / Share Price
= £1.00 / £15.00
= 6.67%

Part (b) - New DPS and Share Valuation (5 marks)

New DPS at 60% payout:
New DPS = EPS × 60%
= £2.50 × 60%
= £1.50 per share

Gordon Growth Model:
P0 = D1 / (r - g)

Where:
D0 = £1.50 (new dividend)
g = 8% (growth rate)
r = 12% (required return)

D1 = D0 × (1 + g) = £1.50 × 1.08 = £1.62

P0 = £1.62 / (0.12 - 0.08)
= £1.62 / 0.04
P0 = £40.50

Assumptions:

  • Dividends grow at a constant rate indefinitely
  • Required return (r) is greater than growth rate (g)
  • The growth rate remains sustainable with the new payout ratio

Part (c) - Share Buybacks (4 marks)

How buybacks return value:

In a share buyback, the company uses cash to repurchase its own shares from the market. This reduces the number of shares outstanding, which increases earnings per share (EPS) and often the share price. Shareholders who sell receive cash directly; those who don't sell own a larger percentage of the company.

Advantage 1 - Tax Efficiency:

In many jurisdictions, capital gains (from share price appreciation following buybacks) are taxed at lower rates than dividend income, or can be deferred until shares are sold. This makes buybacks more tax-efficient for shareholders.

Advantage 2 - Flexibility:

Buybacks are not a commitment like dividends. Companies can adjust or suspend buyback programs without the negative signal associated with dividend cuts. This provides flexibility to respond to changing business conditions or investment opportunities.

Other valid advantages: Signal that management believes shares are undervalued; offset dilution from stock options; shareholders can choose whether to participate.

Part (d) - Signalling Effect and Reluctance to Cut (3 marks)

Signalling Effect:

Dividend changes convey information about management's view of the company's future prospects. Managers have superior information (information asymmetry). A dividend increase signals management's confidence in sustainable future earnings. A dividend cut signals potential financial difficulties or reduced future cash flows. Markets typically react positively to increases and negatively to cuts.

Why managers are reluctant to cut:

Managers avoid dividend cuts because they trigger negative market reactions (share price falls), damage the company's reputation for stability, may cause investor exodus (especially income-focused investors), and are seen as an admission of failure. To avoid these consequences, managers tend to "smooth" dividends, only increasing when they are confident the higher level is sustainable, and cutting only as a last resort.
