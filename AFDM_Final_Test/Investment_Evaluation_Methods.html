Net Present Value (NPV)

The Gold Standard of Investment Appraisal

Formula:
NPV = Σ [Cash Flowt / (1 + r)t] - Initial Investment

Or simply: NPV = Total PV of Inflows - Initial Investment

Decision Rule

  • Independent projects: Accept if NPV > 0
  • Mutually exclusive: Choose the project with highest positive NPV
  • NPV = 0 means project earns exactly the required return (indifferent)

Advantages

  • Measures absolute value creation: Shows the actual pound amount added to shareholder wealth
  • Considers time value of money: Properly discounts all future cash flows
  • Considers all cash flows: Includes entire project life, not just payback period
  • Consistent reinvestment assumption: Assumes reinvestment at cost of capital (realistic)
  • Additive property: NPVs of projects can be added together for portfolio analysis
  • Theoretically correct: Directly linked to shareholder wealth maximization objective
  • No multiple solutions: Always gives a single, unambiguous answer

Disadvantages

  • Requires accurate cost of capital: Discount rate must be estimated correctly - errors significantly impact results
  • Absolute measure: Doesn't show return rate - harder to compare projects of different scales
  • Cash flow estimation: Relies on accurate forecasting of future cash flows
  • Difficult to explain: Non-finance managers may find percentage returns more intuitive
  • Ignores project size: A £1m NPV from a £100m investment isn't shown as worse than £500k from £1m
  • Assumes unlimited capital: May recommend large projects when capital is constrained

When to Use NPV

  • Primary method for all capital budgeting decisions
  • When you need to maximize total shareholder wealth
  • When comparing mutually exclusive projects (always choose highest NPV)
  • When conflicts arise between methods - NPV takes precedence

Exam Tip

Always state that NPV is theoretically superior because it directly measures shareholder wealth creation. When asked "which method should be used?", NPV is almost always the answer. Remember: "NPV is the gold standard of investment appraisal."

Internal Rate of Return (IRR)

The Discount Rate That Makes NPV Zero

Definition: IRR is the discount rate where NPV = 0

Linear Interpolation Formula:
IRR = r1 + [NPV1 / (NPV1 - NPV2)] × (r2 - r1)

Where r1 gives positive NPV and r2 gives negative NPV

Decision Rule

  • Independent projects: Accept if IRR > Cost of Capital (WACC)
  • Mutually exclusive: Choose highest IRR, BUT verify with NPV if projects differ in scale/timing
  • IRR = Cost of Capital means project just breaks even (NPV = 0)

Advantages

  • Percentage return: Easy to understand and compare - "this project returns 15%"
  • Intuitive for managers: Can be compared directly to borrowing costs or required returns
  • No need to specify discount rate upfront: Useful when cost of capital is uncertain
  • Considers time value of money: All cash flows are properly weighted
  • Considers all cash flows: Entire project life is included
  • Benchmarking: Easy to compare against hurdle rates or competing investments

Disadvantages

  • Multiple IRRs: Non-conventional cash flows (sign changes) can produce multiple IRRs or no real IRR
  • Unrealistic reinvestment assumption: Assumes cash flows are reinvested at the IRR itself - often unrealistic for high IRR projects
  • Scale ignorance: A 50% IRR on £1,000 looks better than 20% IRR on £1,000,000, but creates less value
  • Timing conflicts: May rank projects differently than NPV when timing of cash flows differs
  • Can mislead for mutually exclusive projects: Higher IRR doesn't always mean higher value
  • Cannot handle changing discount rates: Assumes flat yield curve over project life

The Multiple IRR Problem

When cash flows change sign more than once (e.g., initial outflow, then inflows, then another outflow for cleanup costs), there can be multiple IRRs or no real IRR. This makes interpretation impossible.

Example: Year 0: -£100, Year 1: +£230, Year 2: -£132 → Two IRRs: 10% and 20%

Solution: Use NPV or Modified IRR (MIRR) instead.

Exam Tip

When calculating IRR by interpolation: (1) Find two rates - one giving positive NPV, one giving negative; (2) The rates should be close together (5% apart is ideal); (3) Apply the formula. Always state: "Since IRR (X%) > Cost of Capital (Y%), accept the project."

Payback Period

Time to Recover Initial Investment

Formula:
Payback Period = Last year with negative cumulative CF + (Remaining amount / Next year's CF)

Example: If cumulative CF at Year 2 = -£200k and Year 3 CF = £500k
Payback = 2 + (200,000 / 500,000) = 2.4 years

Decision Rule

  • Accept if: Payback Period < Target/Benchmark Period
  • Mutually exclusive: Choose project with shortest payback
  • Target is typically set by management (e.g., 3 years, 5 years)

Advantages

  • Simple to calculate: Easy to understand, no complex discounting
  • Easy to communicate: "We get our money back in 3 years" - intuitive for all managers
  • Emphasizes liquidity: Favours projects that return cash quickly
  • Risk screening: Shorter payback = less time for things to go wrong
  • Useful for uncertain environments: When long-term forecasting is unreliable
  • Quick initial filter: Eliminates obviously poor projects before detailed analysis

Disadvantages

  • Ignores time value of money: £1 today treated same as £1 in 5 years
  • Ignores cash flows after payback: A project might have huge returns after payback that are ignored
  • Arbitrary target: No theoretical basis for choosing 3 years vs 4 years
  • Doesn't measure profitability: A project can payback quickly but have low total returns
  • Ignores project scale: £10k project and £10m project with same payback treated equally
  • Bias against long-term projects: R&D, infrastructure projects unfairly penalized

When to Use Payback

  • As a preliminary screening tool, not primary decision criterion
  • In highly uncertain environments where long-term forecasts are unreliable
  • When liquidity/cash flow is a critical constraint
  • For small projects where detailed NPV analysis isn't cost-effective

Exam Tip

Always mention that payback is a "supplementary" method, not a replacement for NPV. Key criticisms: (1) ignores TVM, (2) ignores cash flows after payback. If asked about limitations, these two are essential.

Discounted Payback Period

Payback Using Present Values

Process:
1. Discount each year's cash flow to present value
2. Calculate cumulative discounted cash flows
3. Find when cumulative DCF becomes positive

Formula:
DPB = Last year with negative cumulative DCF + (Remaining / Next year's Discounted CF)

Decision Rule

  • Accept if: Discounted Payback < Target Period
  • DPB will always be longer than simple payback (or equal if r = 0)
  • Some projects may never achieve discounted payback (negative NPV projects)

Advantages

  • Considers time value of money: Cash flows are properly discounted
  • More realistic than simple payback: Accounts for opportunity cost of capital
  • Still emphasizes liquidity: Maintains focus on quick cash recovery
  • Identifies break-even in real terms: When do we recover in today's money?
  • Simple interpretation: Still intuitive for managers

Disadvantages

  • Still ignores cash flows after payback: Same fundamental flaw as simple payback
  • Arbitrary target: No theoretical basis for the benchmark period
  • May never be achieved: Negative NPV projects have infinite discounted payback
  • Doesn't measure total profitability: Ignores value created beyond payback
  • More complex than simple payback: Loses simplicity advantage

Exam Tip

In calculations, show the cumulative discounted cash flow column clearly. Common scenario: DPB exceeds target BUT NPV is positive - state that NPV should take precedence as it measures total value creation.

Accounting Rate of Return (ARR)

Return on Investment Using Accounting Profits

Formula:
ARR = (Average Annual Accounting Profit / Average Investment) × 100%

Where:
Average Investment = (Initial Investment + Scrap Value) / 2
Average Annual Profit = (Total Cash Flows - Total Depreciation) / Number of Years
Total Depreciation = (Initial Investment - Scrap Value)

Decision Rule

  • Accept if: ARR > Target/Required ARR
  • Mutually exclusive: Choose project with highest ARR (above target)
  • Target ARR often linked to company's existing ROCE or ROI

Advantages

  • Uses familiar accounting data: Based on profit figures managers understand
  • Consistent with performance measures: Links to ROI, ROCE used in performance evaluation
  • Considers entire project life: All years' profits included
  • Percentage return: Easy to compare across projects and companies
  • Simple calculation: No discounting required
  • Aligns with financial reporting: Helps predict impact on reported profits

Disadvantages

  • Ignores time value of money: Treats £1 profit in Year 1 same as Year 10
  • Uses profits not cash flows: Profits can be manipulated; depreciation methods affect result
  • Ignores timing of profits: Averaging loses timing information
  • Arbitrary definitions: "Average investment" calculated differently by different companies
  • Not theoretically linked to value: Higher ARR doesn't necessarily mean more shareholder value
  • Ignores working capital: Changes in receivables/inventory not captured

When to Use ARR

  • When managers' bonuses are tied to accounting returns (practical reality)
  • To assess impact on reported financial performance
  • As a supplementary measure alongside NPV
  • When cash flow data is not available

Exam Tip

Be careful with the "Average Investment" calculation - exam questions often specify whether to include scrap value. If in doubt, use (Initial + Scrap) / 2. Key criticism: "uses accounting profits which are subject to accounting policies and ignore cash flow timing."

Profitability Index (PI)

Value Created Per Pound Invested

Formula:
PI = Present Value of Future Cash Flows / Initial Investment

Alternative: PI = 1 + (NPV / Initial Investment)

If NPV = £100,000 and Investment = £500,000: PI = 1 + (100,000/500,000) = 1.20

Decision Rule

  • Accept if: PI > 1.0 (equivalent to NPV > 0)
  • Capital rationing: Rank by PI and select highest until capital exhausted
  • PI = 1.0 means project breaks even (NPV = 0)

Advantages

  • Capital rationing: Perfect for selecting projects when capital is limited
  • Relative measure: Shows value per unit of investment
  • Considers time value: Based on discounted cash flows
  • Considers all cash flows: Entire project life included
  • Consistent with NPV: PI > 1 always means NPV > 0
  • Easy comparison: Useful when projects have different scales

Disadvantages

  • Doesn't measure absolute value: A PI of 2.0 on £100 beats PI of 1.5 on £1m by this measure, but creates less value
  • May conflict with NPV: For mutually exclusive projects, highest PI may not have highest NPV
  • Divisibility assumption: Assumes projects can be partially accepted (often not true)
  • Requires cost of capital: Same estimation challenge as NPV
  • Less intuitive than IRR: "PI of 1.2" less meaningful than "IRR of 20%"

When to Use PI

  • Capital rationing situations: When you have limited funds and must choose among projects
  • To rank independent projects when not all can be undertaken
  • As a supplement to NPV analysis

Exam Tip

PI is especially relevant in capital rationing questions. Rank projects by PI, then select from highest to lowest until budget exhausted. Remember: for mutually exclusive projects, still use NPV as the primary criterion.

Equivalent Annual Annuity (EAA)

Comparing Projects with Different Lifespans

Formula:
EAA = NPV × [r / (1 - (1 + r)-n)]

Or: EAA = NPV / PVIFA(r, n)

This converts a lump-sum NPV into an equivalent annual amount

Decision Rule

  • For mutually exclusive projects: Choose project with highest EAA
  • Only use when projects have different useful lives and are repeatable
  • Higher EAA = more annual value creation

Advantages

  • Handles different project lives: Makes fair comparison between 3-year and 5-year projects
  • Based on NPV: Retains theoretical soundness of NPV approach
  • Annual measure: Easy to understand as "annual value created"
  • Avoids replacement chain analysis: Simpler than extending projects to common life

Disadvantages

  • Assumes repeatability: Projects must be repeatable indefinitely at same NPV
  • Ignores technological change: Future replacement projects may be different
  • Cash flow timing: Converts to annuity, losing specific timing information
  • Limited applicability: Only useful for specific scenario (different lives + repeatability)
  • Assumes constant conditions: Same cost of capital, inflation, etc. forever

Exam Tip

EAA is specifically for comparing mutually exclusive projects with different useful lives. Calculate NPV for each project first, then convert to EAA. Always state the assumption that projects can be repeated.

NPV vs IRR: When They Conflict

For independent projects, NPV and IRR always give the same accept/reject decision. However, for mutually exclusive projects, they may rank differently.

Why Conflicts Occur

  1. Scale Difference: A smaller project may have higher IRR but lower NPV
    Example: Project A (£10k investment, IRR 50%, NPV £3k) vs Project B (£1m investment, IRR 20%, NPV £150k)
  2. Timing Difference: Projects with earlier cash flows are favoured by IRR
    IRR's reinvestment assumption inflates the value of early returns
  3. Reinvestment Rate Assumption: NPV assumes reinvestment at cost of capital; IRR assumes reinvestment at IRR itself

Resolution: NPV Wins

When NPV and IRR give conflicting rankings, always follow NPV because:

  • NPV directly measures shareholder wealth creation (the primary corporate objective)
  • NPV's reinvestment assumption (at cost of capital) is more realistic
  • NPV considers project scale and absolute value creation

Key Exam Answer Pattern

When asked to discuss NPV vs IRR conflicts, always structure your answer:

  1. State that for independent projects, both methods give same accept/reject decision
  2. Explain that conflicts arise for mutually exclusive projects
  3. Give reasons: scale differences, timing differences, reinvestment assumptions
  4. Conclude: NPV should be preferred as it directly measures shareholder wealth
