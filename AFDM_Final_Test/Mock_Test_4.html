SECTION A - COMPULSORY QUESTION (30 Marks)

Question 1

NovaTech Solutions Ltd is a technology company considering financing options for a major expansion. The company currently has the following capital structure:

  • 4 million ordinary shares currently trading at £6.50 per share
  • The company just paid a dividend of £0.45 per share, with dividends expected to grow at 6% per annum indefinitely
  • £8 million in 7% coupon bonds (face value £100 each), currently trading at £92, with 6 years to maturity
  • Corporate tax rate: 30%

The company is evaluating a new AI software project requiring an initial investment of £5 million. The project is expected to generate the following annual cash flows:

YearCash Flow (£)
1800,000
21,400,000
31,800,000
41,600,000
51,200,000

Required:

  1. Calculate the company's cost of equity using the Dividend Growth Model. (4 Marks)
  2. Calculate the yield to maturity (YTM) of the bonds using linear interpolation, and determine the after-tax cost of debt. (6 Marks)
  3. Calculate the company's Weighted Average Cost of Capital (WACC). (6 Marks)
  4. Using the WACC calculated above, determine the Net Present Value (NPV) of the AI software project. Should the project be accepted? (8 Marks)
  5. Explain TWO advantages and TWO disadvantages of using debt financing compared to equity financing. (6 Marks)

Total: 30 Marks

Model Answer - Question 1

Part (a) - Cost of Equity (4 marks)

Dividend Growth Model: Ke = (D1 / P0) + g

D0 = £0.45 (dividend just paid)
g = 6% = 0.06
P0 = £6.50

D1 = D0 × (1 + g) = £0.45 × 1.06 = £0.477

Ke = (0.477 / 6.50) + 0.06
Ke = 0.0734 + 0.06
Ke = 13.34%

Part (b) - YTM and After-tax Cost of Debt (6 marks)

Given: Coupon = 7%, Face Value = £100, Price = £92, n = 6 years
Annual Coupon = £100 × 7% = £7

Since Price (£92) < Face Value (£100), YTM > Coupon Rate (7%)

Try r = 8%:
PV = 7 × PVIFA(8%,6) + 100 × PVIF(8%,6)
PV = 7 × 4.623 + 100 × 0.630 = 32.36 + 63.00 = £95.36

Try r = 9%:
PV = 7 × PVIFA(9%,6) + 100 × PVIF(9%,6)
PV = 7 × 4.486 + 100 × 0.596 = 31.40 + 59.60 = £91.00

Linear Interpolation:
YTM = 8% + [(95.36 - 92) / (95.36 - 91.00)] × (9% - 8%)
YTM = 8% + [3.36 / 4.36] × 1%
YTM = 8% + 0.77%
YTM = 8.77%

After-tax Cost of Debt:
Kd(1-T) = 8.77% × (1 - 0.30) = 8.77% × 0.70
Kd(1-T) = 6.14%

Part (c) - WACC Calculation (6 marks)

Market Values:
Equity (E) = 4,000,000 shares × £6.50 = £26,000,000
Debt (D) = (£8,000,000 / £100) × £92 = 80,000 bonds × £92 = £7,360,000
Total (V) = £26,000,000 + £7,360,000 = £33,360,000

Weights:
We = 26,000,000 / 33,360,000 = 0.7794 (77.94%)
Wd = 7,360,000 / 33,360,000 = 0.2206 (22.06%)

WACC:
WACC = (We × Ke) + (Wd × Kd(1-T))
WACC = (0.7794 × 13.34%) + (0.2206 × 6.14%)
WACC = 10.40% + 1.35%
WACC = 11.75%

Part (e) - Debt vs Equity Financing (6 marks)

Advantages of Debt Financing:

  1. Tax Shield: Interest payments are tax-deductible, reducing the effective cost of debt and the company's tax liability.
  2. No Dilution of Ownership: Debt does not dilute existing shareholders' ownership or voting control, unlike issuing new shares.

Disadvantages of Debt Financing:

  1. Fixed Obligations: Interest and principal must be paid regardless of profitability, increasing financial risk and potential bankruptcy if cash flows are insufficient.
  2. Restrictive Covenants: Lenders often impose restrictions on company activities (dividend payments, additional borrowing, asset sales), limiting managerial flexibility.
SECTION B - ANSWER ANY TWO QUESTIONS (15 Marks Each)

Question 2 - Time Value of Money

You are a financial advisor helping clients with various investment and loan decisions.

Required:

  1. Your client wants to save for retirement. They plan to deposit £5,000 at the end of each year for the next 25 years into an account earning 7% per annum. Calculate the future value of these savings at retirement. (4 marks)
  2. Another client has won a lottery prize that offers two payment options:
    • Option A: £500,000 lump sum today
    • Option B: £60,000 per year for 12 years, starting one year from now
    If the discount rate is 8%, which option should the client choose? Show your calculations. (5 marks)
  3. A business client needs a loan of £200,000 to be repaid over 10 years with equal annual payments at 6% interest. Calculate the annual payment required. (3 marks)
  4. Explain the difference between an ordinary annuity and an annuity due. How does this affect the present value calculation? (3 marks)

Total: 15 Marks

Model Answer - Question 2

Part (a) - Future Value of Annuity (4 marks)

Future Value of Ordinary Annuity:
FVA = PMT × [(1 + r)^n - 1] / r

PMT = £5,000, r = 7% = 0.07, n = 25 years

FVA = 5,000 × [(1.07)^25 - 1] / 0.07
FVA = 5,000 × [5.4274 - 1] / 0.07
FVA = 5,000 × 4.4274 / 0.07
FVA = 5,000 × 63.249
FVA = £316,245

Part (b) - Lottery Options Comparison (5 marks)

Option A: PV = £500,000 (already in present value)

Option B: Present Value of Ordinary Annuity
PVA = PMT × [1 - (1 + r)^-n] / r
PVA = 60,000 × [1 - (1.08)^-12] / 0.08
PVA = 60,000 × [1 - 0.3971] / 0.08
PVA = 60,000 × 0.6029 / 0.08
PVA = 60,000 × 7.536
PVA = £452,160
Decision: Option A (£500,000) > Option B (£452,160). Choose Option A - the lump sum is worth more in present value terms.

Part (c) - Loan Payment Calculation (3 marks)

Annuity Payment Formula:
PV = PMT × [1 - (1 + r)^-n] / r
Rearranging: PMT = PV × r / [1 - (1 + r)^-n]

PV = £200,000, r = 6% = 0.06, n = 10 years

PMT = 200,000 × 0.06 / [1 - (1.06)^-10]
PMT = 12,000 / [1 - 0.5584]
PMT = 12,000 / 0.4416
PMT = £27,174 per year

Part (d) - Ordinary Annuity vs Annuity Due (3 marks)

Ordinary Annuity: Payments occur at the END of each period. Most loans and savings plans use this structure.

Annuity Due: Payments occur at the BEGINNING of each period. Rent and insurance premiums typically follow this pattern.

Effect on Present Value: An annuity due has a HIGHER present value than an ordinary annuity with the same payments, because each payment is received one period earlier. The relationship is:

PV (Annuity Due) = PV (Ordinary Annuity) × (1 + r)

Question 3 - Portfolio Risk and Return

An investor is considering creating a portfolio using two stocks: Alpha Corp and Beta Ltd. The following information is available:

Stock Expected Return Standard Deviation Portfolio Weight
Alpha Corp 14% 20% 60%
Beta Ltd 9% 12% 40%

The correlation coefficient between the two stocks is 0.3.

Required:

  1. Calculate the expected return of the portfolio. (2 marks)
  2. Calculate the standard deviation of the portfolio. (5 marks)
  3. Explain what the correlation coefficient of 0.3 tells us about the relationship between these two stocks and its implications for diversification. (4 marks)
  4. If the correlation coefficient were -0.5 instead of 0.3, would the portfolio risk be higher or lower? Explain your reasoning without recalculating. (4 marks)

Total: 15 Marks

Model Answer - Question 3

Part (a) - Portfolio Expected Return (2 marks)

E(Rp) = wA × E(RA) + wB × E(RB)

E(Rp) = (0.60 × 14%) + (0.40 × 9%)
E(Rp) = 8.4% + 3.6%
E(Rp) = 12%

Part (b) - Portfolio Standard Deviation (5 marks)

Portfolio Variance Formula:
σp² = wA²σA² + wB²σB² + 2×wA×wB×σA×σB×ρAB

Where:
wA = 0.60, wB = 0.40
σA = 20% = 0.20, σB = 12% = 0.12
ρAB = 0.3

σp² = (0.60)²(0.20)² + (0.40)²(0.12)² + 2(0.60)(0.40)(0.20)(0.12)(0.3)
σp² = (0.36)(0.04) + (0.16)(0.0144) + 2(0.60)(0.40)(0.20)(0.12)(0.3)
σp² = 0.0144 + 0.002304 + 0.003456
σp² = 0.02016

σp = √0.02016 = 0.1420
Portfolio Standard Deviation = 14.20%
Note: The portfolio risk (14.20%) is less than the weighted average of individual risks (0.6×20% + 0.4×12% = 16.8%), demonstrating diversification benefits.

Part (c) - Correlation Coefficient Interpretation (4 marks)

What ρ = 0.3 means:

  • The correlation coefficient of 0.3 indicates a weak positive relationship between the returns of Alpha Corp and Beta Ltd.
  • When Alpha's returns increase, Beta's returns tend to increase slightly as well, but the relationship is not strong.
  • The stocks do not move perfectly together (ρ ≠ 1) nor in opposite directions (ρ ≠ -1).

Diversification implications:

  • Since ρ < 1, combining these stocks provides diversification benefits.
  • The portfolio risk is reduced below the weighted average of individual risks.
  • The lower the correlation, the greater the diversification benefit. With ρ = 0.3, moderate diversification is achieved.

Part (d) - Effect of Negative Correlation (4 marks)

If ρ = -0.5: Portfolio risk would be LOWER.

Reasoning:

  • A negative correlation means the stocks tend to move in opposite directions - when one rises, the other tends to fall.
  • This provides superior diversification because losses in one stock are partially offset by gains in the other.
  • In the variance formula, the covariance term (2×wA×wB×σA×σB×ρAB) becomes negative when ρ is negative, reducing overall portfolio variance.
  • The more negative the correlation, the greater the risk reduction through diversification.

Question 4 - Equity Valuation and Rights Issue

Stellar Dynamics Plc is a growing technology company with 10 million ordinary shares in issue, currently trading at £8 per share. The company paid a dividend of £0.60 per share last year. Analysts expect dividends to grow at 15% per year for the next 3 years, then stabilize at 4% growth indefinitely.

To fund expansion, the company is considering a 1-for-4 rights issue at a 20% discount to the current market price.

Required:

  1. Using the two-stage dividend growth model, calculate the theoretical value of one share if the required return is 12%. (6 marks)
  2. Calculate the theoretical ex-rights price (TERP) per share after the rights issue. (4 marks)
  3. Calculate the value of one right. (2 marks)
  4. Explain the THREE options available to a shareholder when offered a rights issue. (3 marks)

Total: 15 Marks

Model Answer - Question 4

Part (a) - Two-Stage Dividend Growth Model (6 marks)

Stage 1: High growth period (Years 1-3, g = 15%)
D0 = £0.60
D1 = 0.60 × 1.15 = £0.69
D2 = 0.69 × 1.15 = £0.7935
D3 = 0.7935 × 1.15 = £0.9125

PV of Stage 1 dividends (at r = 12%):
PV1 = 0.69 / 1.12 = £0.6161
PV2 = 0.7935 / (1.12)² = £0.6325
PV3 = 0.9125 / (1.12)³ = £0.6494
Total PV Stage 1 = £1.898

Stage 2: Constant growth (Year 4 onwards, g = 4%)
D4 = D3 × (1 + g) = 0.9125 × 1.04 = £0.949

Terminal Value at end of Year 3 (using Gordon Growth Model):
P3 = D4 / (r - g) = 0.949 / (0.12 - 0.04) = 0.949 / 0.08 = £11.8625

PV of Terminal Value:
PV(P3) = 11.8625 / (1.12)³ = 11.8625 / 1.4049 = £8.4434

Total Share Value:
P0 = PV Stage 1 + PV Terminal Value
P0 = £1.898 + £8.4434
P0 = £10.34

Part (b) - Theoretical Ex-Rights Price (4 marks)

Rights Issue Details:
1-for-4 rights issue = 1 new share for every 4 existing shares
Discount = 20%, so Issue Price = £8 × (1 - 0.20) = £6.40

TERP Calculation:
TERP = (Value of old shares + Value of new shares) / Total shares

For every 4 old shares + 1 new share:
TERP = (4 × £8 + 1 × £6.40) / (4 + 1)
TERP = (£32 + £6.40) / 5
TERP = £38.40 / 5
TERP = £7.68

Part (c) - Value of One Right (2 marks)

Value of Right = TERP - Issue Price
= £7.68 - £6.40
= £1.28

Alternative: Value per existing share = (Current Price - TERP) = £8 - £7.68 = £0.32
Since 4 rights needed for 1 new share: 4 × £0.32 = £1.28

Part (d) - Shareholder Options (3 marks)

Option 1 - Take up the rights (subscribe):

The shareholder pays the issue price to buy the new shares. Their total investment increases, but they maintain their proportional ownership. Wealth is preserved as the discount is captured through the lower purchase price.

Option 2 - Sell the rights (renounce):

The shareholder sells the rights in the market to another investor. They receive cash (the value of the rights) but their ownership percentage decreases. Theoretically, wealth is preserved as the cash received compensates for the dilution.

Option 3 - Do nothing (let rights lapse):

The shareholder ignores the rights offer. Their ownership is diluted AND they receive no compensation. This results in a loss of wealth equal to the value of the rights. This is the worst option and should be avoided.
