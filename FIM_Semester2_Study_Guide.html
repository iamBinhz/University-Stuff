Week 1

Alternative Funds

Types of Alternative Investments

Fund Type Description Key Characteristics
Hedge Funds Pooled investment funds using diverse strategies High fees (2% + 20%), accredited investors only, less regulated
Private Equity Investments in private companies Long lock-up periods, leveraged buyouts, illiquid
REITs Real Estate Investment Trusts Must distribute 90% of income, publicly traded
Venture Capital Early-stage company investments High risk/high return, active management
Infrastructure Roads, utilities, airports Stable cash flows, inflation protection

Hedge Fund Strategies

  • Long/Short Equity: Buy undervalued, short overvalued stocks
  • Market Neutral: Equal long and short positions to eliminate market risk
  • Global Macro: Bet on macroeconomic trends across countries
  • Event-Driven: Exploit corporate events (mergers, bankruptcies)
  • Arbitrage: Exploit price differences in related securities
Illiquidity Premium: Alternative assets offer higher returns to compensate for lack of liquidity. Pension funds with long-term horizons can capture this premium.
Hidden Risks: Stale pricing (quarterly valuations), return smoothing (artificially low volatility), denominator effect during market crashes.
Week 2

Interest Rates

Types of Interest Rates

Rate Type Definition
Nominal Rate Stated rate before adjusting for inflation
Real Rate Nominal rate minus inflation rate
Risk-Free Rate Return on government securities (no default risk)
Base Rate Central bank's policy rate (Bank Rate in UK)
Fisher Equation
Real Interest Rate = Nominal Rate - Inflation Rate
r = i - π

Term Structure of Interest Rates

The relationship between interest rates and time to maturity.

Yield Curve Shapes

Shape Meaning Economic Signal
Normal (Upward) Long-term rates > Short-term rates Economic expansion expected
Inverted (Downward) Short-term rates > Long-term rates Recession indicator
Flat Rates similar across maturities Economic uncertainty
Humped Medium-term rates highest Transition period

Theories of Term Structure

  1. Expectations Theory: Long-term rates = average of expected future short-term rates
  2. Liquidity Premium Theory: Investors demand premium for holding longer maturities
  3. Market Segmentation Theory: Different investors prefer different maturities
Yield Curve Inversion: When short-term rates exceed long-term rates, historically predicts recession within 12-18 months. Evidence is strong but not perfect.
Week 3

Central Banks

Functions of Central Banks

  • Monetary Policy: Control money supply and interest rates
  • Lender of Last Resort: Provide emergency liquidity to banks
  • Financial Stability: Monitor systemic risks
  • Bank Supervision: Regulate banking sector
  • Currency Issuance: Sole authority to issue banknotes
  • Government's Bank: Manage government accounts and debt

Bank of England Structure

Committee Responsibility
MPC (Monetary Policy Committee) Sets Bank Rate to meet 2% inflation target
FPC (Financial Policy Committee) Macroprudential regulation, systemic risk
PRA (Prudential Regulation Authority) Microprudential supervision of banks

Monetary Policy Tools

Tool How It Works
Bank Rate Interest rate BoE pays on reserves; influences all other rates
Open Market Operations Buy/sell government securities to adjust money supply
Quantitative Easing (QE) Large-scale asset purchases when rates near zero
Reserve Requirements Minimum reserves banks must hold (0% in US since 2020)
Forward Guidance Communication about future policy intentions
Central Bank Independence: Insulates monetary policy from political pressure. Leads to lower, more stable inflation.

Risk Management

Types of Financial Risk

Risk Type Definition Example
Credit Risk Borrower fails to repay Loan default
Market Risk Losses from market price changes Stock price decline, interest rate changes
Liquidity Risk Unable to sell assets or meet obligations Bank run, fire sale
Operational Risk Failures in processes, systems, people Fraud, IT failure, human error
Systemic Risk Failure spreads across financial system 2008 financial crisis

Risk Measurement

Value at Risk (VaR)
Maximum expected loss over a given time period at a given confidence level

Example: "1-day 95% VaR of £1 million" means:
95% confident losses won't exceed £1 million in one day

Basel III Capital Requirements

Requirement Minimum
Common Equity Tier 1 (CET1) 4.5% of RWA
Tier 1 Capital 6% of RWA
Total Capital 8% of RWA
Capital Conservation Buffer +2.5%
Countercyclical Buffer 0-2.5%
Risk-Weighted Assets (RWA): Assets weighted by riskiness. Cash = 0%, Government bonds = 0-20%, Mortgages = 35-50%, Corporate loans = 100%+

Money Markets

Characteristics

  • Short-term instruments (maturity < 1 year)
  • High liquidity
  • Low default risk
  • Large denominations (wholesale market)

Money Market Instruments

Instrument Issuer Maturity
Treasury Bills (T-Bills) Government 4, 13, 26, 52 weeks
Commercial Paper Corporations 1-270 days
Certificates of Deposit (CDs) Banks 1-12 months
Repurchase Agreements (Repos) Various Overnight to weeks
Banker's Acceptances Banks (for trade) 30-180 days
LIBOR/SONIA Interbank rate Overnight to 12 months

Repurchase Agreements (Repos)

Short-term collateralized loan:

  1. Seller sells securities to buyer
  2. Seller agrees to repurchase at higher price
  3. Difference = interest (repo rate)
T-Bill Discount Yield
Discount Yield = [(Face Value - Price) / Face Value] × (360 / Days to Maturity)
SONIA (Sterling Overnight Index Average): Replaced LIBOR as UK benchmark rate. Based on actual overnight transactions.
Week 6

Bond Markets

Bond Basics

Term Definition
Face Value (Par) Amount repaid at maturity (typically £100 or £1,000)
Coupon Rate Annual interest payment as % of face value
Yield to Maturity (YTM) Total return if held to maturity
Current Yield Annual coupon / Current price

Types of Bonds

Type Characteristics
Government Bonds (Gilts) Low risk, benchmark rate, highly liquid
Corporate Bonds Higher yield, credit risk, various ratings
Municipal Bonds Local government, often tax-exempt
Zero-Coupon Bonds No coupons, sold at discount, single payment at maturity
Index-Linked Bonds Payments adjusted for inflation
Bond Price
Price = Σ [Coupon / (1+r)^t] + [Face Value / (1+r)^n]

Where: r = yield, t = period, n = total periods

Bond Price and Yield Relationship

Inverse Relationship: When interest rates rise, bond prices fall (and vice versa). This is because existing bonds with lower coupons become less attractive.

Grade S&P/Fitch Moody's Meaning

BBB Baa Medium grade
Speculative (Junk) BB, B Ba, B Speculative
CCC-D Caa-C High risk/Default
Duration
Measures bond price sensitivity to interest rate changes

Higher duration = Greater price volatility
Longer maturity & lower coupon = Higher duration
Week 7

Foreign Exchange Markets - Part 1

Forex Market Overview

  • Largest financial market (~$7.5 trillion daily volume)
  • 24-hour trading (follows the sun: Asia → Europe → Americas)
  • Over-the-counter (OTC) decentralized market
  • No physical location - electronic network of banks and dealers

Market Participants

Participant Role
Commercial Banks Primary dealers, provide liquidity, trade for clients
Central Banks Manage reserves, intervene to stabilize currency
Corporations Trade for business needs (imports/exports, hedging)
Hedge Funds Speculative trading for profit
Retail Traders Individual speculators (small % of market)

Exchange Rate Basics

Definition: The price of one currency in terms of another.

Term Meaning Example (UK perspective)
Base Currency First currency in pair GBP in GBP/USD
Quote Currency Second currency in pair USD in GBP/USD
Direct Quote Domestic per unit of foreign £0.80 per $1
Indirect Quote Foreign per unit of domestic $1.25 per £1

Bid-Ask Spread

Rate Definition Who Uses
Bid Price Price dealer BUYS base currency You sell at bid (lower price)
Ask Price Price dealer SELLS base currency You buy at ask (higher price)
Spread Ask - Bid = Dealer's profit Narrower = more liquid market
Bid-Ask Spread Percentage
Spread % = [(Ask - Bid) / Ask] × 100

Exchange Rate Regimes

Regime Description Examples
Free Float Market determines rate, no intervention GBP, USD, EUR, JPY
Managed Float Market-based with occasional CB intervention Many emerging markets
Fixed/Pegged Tied to another currency or basket Hong Kong Dollar (to USD)
Currency Board Fixed rate backed by foreign reserves Bulgaria

Factors Affecting Exchange Rates

  • Interest Rate Differentials: Higher rates attract capital → currency appreciates
  • Inflation Differentials: Higher inflation → currency depreciates (PPP)
  • Current Account Balance: Surplus → demand for currency → appreciates
  • Economic Growth: Strong growth attracts investment → appreciates
  • Political Stability: Uncertainty causes capital flight → depreciates
  • Speculation: Market expectations and sentiment
Appreciation vs Depreciation:
If GBP/USD rises from 1.25 to 1.30 → GBP appreciated (buys more USD)
If GBP/USD falls from 1.25 to 1.20 → GBP depreciated (buys less USD)

Cross Rates

Exchange rate between two currencies calculated via a third currency.

Cross Rate Calculation
EUR/GBP = EUR/USD ÷ GBP/USD

Example: If EUR/USD = 1.10 and GBP/USD = 1.25
EUR/GBP = 1.10 ÷ 1.25 = 0.88
Week 8

Foreign Exchange Markets - Part 2

Forex Instruments

Instrument Description
Spot Immediate exchange (T+2 settlement)
Forward Agreement to exchange at future date at set rate
Swap Spot + forward combined
Options Right (not obligation) to exchange at set rate
Interest Rate Parity
Forward Rate / Spot Rate = (1 + r_domestic) / (1 + r_foreign)

If domestic rates > foreign rates → forward rate at premium

Purchasing Power Parity (PPP)

Theory that exchange rates should adjust so that identical goods cost the same in different countries when expressed in a common currency.

Law of One Price

Foundation of PPP: In efficient markets, identical goods must sell for the same price in different locations (when expressed in same currency), otherwise arbitrage would occur.

Law of One Price
P_domestic = S × P_foreign

Where: S = Spot exchange rate (domestic per foreign)

Absolute PPP vs Relative PPP

Type Definition Formula
Absolute PPP Exchange rate equals ratio of price levels S = P_domestic / P_foreign
Relative PPP Exchange rate change equals inflation differential %ΔS ≈ π_domestic - π_foreign
Relative PPP Formula
(S₁ - S₀) / S₀ = (π_domestic - π_foreign)

Where:
S₀ = Current spot rate
S₁ = Expected future spot rate
π = Inflation rate

Interpretation: If UK inflation is 3% and US inflation is 1%, GBP should depreciate by approximately 2% against USD.

Big Mac Index (Real-World PPP Example)

The Economist's informal measure of PPP using McDonald's Big Mac prices worldwide.

Country Big Mac Price Implied PPP Rate Actual Rate Valuation
USA $5.69 - - Baseline
UK £4.19 $1.36/£ $1.25/£ GBP undervalued ~8%
Switzerland CHF 6.90 $0.82/CHF $1.10/CHF CHF overvalued ~34%
Big Mac PPP Calculation
Implied PPP Rate = Big Mac Price (local) / Big Mac Price (US)

Example: £4.19 / $5.69 = 0.736 → Implied rate: $1.36 per £1
If actual rate is $1.25/£, GBP is undervalued by (1.36-1.25)/1.36 = 8%

PPP in Practice: Short-Run vs Long-Run

Time Horizon PPP Validity Reason
Short-run Poor predictor Interest rates, speculation, capital flows dominate
Long-run (5+ years) Better predictor Prices eventually adjust, arbitrage works over time

Limitations of PPP

  • Transportation costs: Goods aren't costless to move across borders
  • Trade barriers: Tariffs, quotas, and regulations prevent arbitrage
  • Non-traded goods: Services (haircuts, rent) can't be arbitraged internationally
  • Product differentiation: A Big Mac in London isn't identical to one in New York (quality, taste preferences)
  • Price stickiness: Prices adjust slowly due to contracts, menu costs
  • Capital flows: Exchange rates often driven by investment, not goods trade
Exam Tip: PPP works better for:
• Long time horizons (years, not days)
• Countries with high inflation differentials
• Tradeable goods (commodities) rather than services
Common Misconception: PPP does NOT predict short-term exchange rate movements. Daily/monthly rates are driven by interest rate differentials, capital flows, and speculation - not inflation differences.
Weeks 9-10

Equity Markets

Why Stock Markets Exist

  • Raise capital for companies
  • Diversify risk for investors
  • Provide liquidity
  • Enable profit through investment
  • NOT: Provide loans (that's debt markets)

Primary vs Secondary Markets

Primary Market Secondary Market
New securities issued (IPO) Existing securities traded
Company receives funds Investors trade with each other
Also called "new issue market" Also called "stock market"

Common vs Preferred Shares

Feature Common Shares Preferred Shares
Voting Rights Yes No
Dividend Priority Lower (residual) Higher (fixed)
Claim on Assets Last (after all others) Before common
Residual Claimants: Common shareholders receive remaining cash flow AFTER all other claims (debt, preferred dividends) are paid.

Stock Valuation

One-Period Valuation Model
P₀ = (D₁ + P₁) / (1 + r)

Value = Present value of expected dividend + Present value of expected selling price
Price-Earnings (P/E) Ratio
P/E = Market Price per Share / Earnings per Share

High P/E → Investors optimistic about future earnings
Low P/E → Company may be undervalued or in trouble
Equity Risk Premium
Equity Risk Premium = Expected Return on Stocks - Risk-Free Rate

Efficient Market Hypothesis (EMH)

Definition: Current price fully reflects all available relevant information.

Form Information Reflected Implication
Weak All past prices Technical analysis useless
Semi-Strong All public information Fundamental analysis useless
Strong All information (public + private) No one can beat market
EMH Implications:
  • Future price changes are unpredictable
  • Unexploited profit opportunities quickly eliminated
  • If news is public, price already adjusted - no abnormal returns

Practice MCQs (Equity Markets)

Test Your Knowledge

1. According to the efficient market hypothesis, the current price of a security:
A) Is the discounted NPV of future interest payments
B) Is determined by the lowest successful bidder
C) Fully reflects all available relevant information
D) None of the above
Answer: C
2. The efficient markets hypothesis implies future stock price changes should be:
A) Unpredictable
B) Set by each country
C) Increasing
D) Pegged to a standard
Answer: A
3. Which is NOT a characteristic of NYSE?
A) Largest US stock exchange
B) Primarily trades stocks
C) Formally established in 1917
D) Operates as auction market
Answer: C (NYSE established 1817, not 1917)
4. Which exchange is known for tech companies and less stringent listing requirements?
A) NYSE
B) LSE
C) TSE
D) NASDAQ
Answer: D
5. How is the DJIA calculated?
A) Price-weighted
B) Market-cap-weighted
C) Equal-weighted
D) Volume-weighted
Answer: A
6. Which is NOT a US index?
A) DJIA
B) NASDAQ 100
C) FTSE 100
D) S&P 500
Answer: C (FTSE 100 is UK)
7. A speculative bubble occurs when:
A) Prices equal intrinsic value
B) Prices fall due to dividends
C) Prices move away from intrinsic value
D) Companies stop trading
Answer: C
8. A high P/E ratio indicates:
A) Irregular dividends
B) Financial instability
C) Investors optimistic about future earnings
D) Declining profits
Answer: C
9. Preferred shareholders do NOT have the right to:
A) Vote
B) Receive dividend
C) Claim over assets
D) Claim over company income
Answer: A
10. Equity risk premium means:
A) Return on bonds minus stocks
B) Return on stocks minus risk-free rate
C) Risk-free rate minus stocks
D) Dividend minus tax
Answer: B
